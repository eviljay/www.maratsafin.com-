July 19, 2001
Los Angeles, U.S.A.

An Interview With Marat Safin
www.atptennis.com

The U.S. Open champ will play in Los Angeles and Montreal for the first time.



Marat Safin returns to the North American hardcourt circuit next Monday, as the No. 2 seed at the Mercedes Benz Cup in Los Angeles. He then heads to the Tennis Masters Series Canada in Montreal, where he's the defending champion. He also will play at the Tennis Masters Cincinnati and RCA Championships in Indianapolis, where he reached the final last year.

In 2000, he compiled a 12-2 record during the U.S. summer hard-court circuit going into the U.S. Open. Last year, Safin finished No. 2, the youngest player to finish No. 2 since Boris Becker in 1986. He had seven ATP titles, and he earned his first Grand Slam title at the U.S. Open with his win over Pete Sampras.

Earlier this year, he suffered a back injury in Dubai, en route to the final. He's coming off a quarter final finish at Wimbledon. He's No. 2 in the ATP Entry System, and 20th in the ATP Champions Race.


Q: Can you tell us about your health and also how you're looking forward to going into the summer circuit, being you had such a great year last year?

SAFIN: Yes, I'm much better than before. I got injured unfortunately at the beginning of the year, just right before Indian Wells and Miami. I wouldn't get any points, and that was bad. I had a difficult time. You never know when you can come back and when you can start to play well. And also when you're coming from an injury, you don't have much confidence, and it's very tough to play again.

Q: You're going into part of the season that you just really dominated last year. How are you feeling, trying to go back and duplicate it or just taking it as it's going to come?

SAFIN: Well, I think now that I'm healthy and everything is perfect. I played well at Wimbledon. I'm happy with this. I'm starting to play better. I hope I can make some good result there. And of course I will go for everything. I would like to win a tournament in America.

Q: Why did you decide to play L.A. this year?

SAFIN: It's a big tournament and I think it's going to be tough, but it's a really good tournament to play in and it's really fun. Everybody told me it's a great. It's good preparation for a big tournament.

Q. Have you ever been to Los Angeles before?

SAFIN: The Los Angeles airport. But I would like to spend a week there.

Q: Have your back problems cleared up completely or is it something you have to monitor and do exercises for?

SAFIN: No, I'm completely recovered. Now the problem is I don't have enough confidence to play big matches, but I'm looking forward. I think it's going to come back, but I think it's a little too late for this year, so I hope everything will be okay for next year.

Q: You just said you've had an up-and-down year and the confidence has sort of come in and out but you had a good result at Wimbledon. Can you give yourself a grade, if you had to give yourself a grade for this year, what would you assess your grade?

SAFIN: It depends a lot on what I'm going to do in the States. It depends a lot on everything. It depends if I make a good result. I can finish everywhere, but if I make some good results, it would be great (inaudible). If everything goes in the right direction, I think it will be great. It will be perfect for me.

Q: How much pressure do you expect or what kind of feeling do you expect going into the U.S. Open this year, being the defending champion?

SAFIN: The expectation is people will recognize me. It would be great. A new experience. I think something new and it's nice, but also it's very important you have control. So it's very important in the first round. If I'm going to be in the second week, I think I can make good results. I would like to make the semis. That would be great.

Q: And what is Mats Wilander doing for you?

SAFIN: I'm really happy to work with him. I would never expect that Mats Wilander would work with me. I'm learning a lot. And I'm happy to be with him, actually. It's a great experience. He's coming to Montreal and we're going to start to work again and I'll be ready for the Open.

Q: Can you tell me where your game is right now compared to where you were last year at this time?

SAFIN: It's not the same level as last year.

Q: About how far off do you think you're are?

SAFIN: I don't know, 60 percent, if I'm lucky. It's now two or three difficult matches and then everything is changing. You start to play better and better and then at the end nobody can touch you. It's very important to win tough matches. And I couldn't make very many good results this year. I didn't win enough tough matches, so it's a little bit difficult. Every time you have a touch match, you have doubts about how you're playing and if you're going to win this match. Last year I didn't have any doubts about me and I was perfect. My condition was perfect. I was full of confidence. I was playing great and actually not many people touched me.

Q: Do you think you can get back to that level before you get to Flushing Meadows?

SAFIN: I have to. I have to get there, and I hope I will. I would like to play well in the U.S. I always played well actually there, and I would like to make the same this year, play well and feel comfortable with everything and I think I can make it.

Q: I just want to know from Marat, the fact that he's coming into Montreal for the first time, is there anything in particular that he's going to try and do to get ready for this tournament? Has he heard anything positive about the venue?

SAFIN: No, it's very important the first round. If you have a good draw, you start to play well and then (inaudible) its difficult to play against me if I'm playing well and I'm perfect, and physical conditions, and mentally I'm prepared. I'll be tough there. I will go for everything. I'll fight like a dog. I will be there. I will jump. I will dive. I will do everything possible to win the tournament.

Q: You said earlier you were at 60 percent, but physically now are you at 100 percent?

SAFIN: Yes, physically I'm perfect, but the problem is that it is not only physical, it's mental and everything together is a player. I have some parts of a player that are key, but not yet.

Q: What do you expect from Montreal? What do you expect your first game on the court will be?

SAFIN: I don't care about anything. I just want to win. It doesn't matter how. If it's going to be an ugly match or nice match, I want to win. I want to beat everybody and I want to get my game like it was last year. I want to get there.

Q: If you look over the last year, how has winning a Grand Slam title affected you most as a person?

SAFIN: Actually, you don't realize this until I think you win the second time. I didn't win the second time, so I don't know how I feel. I feel happy, but I didn't enjoy it enough. I had no time to enjoy it. But the second time, I will win and hopefully it will be this year, maybe, I will enjoy it more and I will -- I can tell you which is better to win the first time or the second time. You need to have experience to enjoy it more.

Q: You mentioned about the confidence. Is it because you couldn't move as well because of your back injury or because you didn't feel as sharp in matches?

SAFIN: No, because when you get injured and you don't play for two months, it's very difficult to come back and win matches right away, because you're not used to playing long matches. You're not used to running a lot. It's very difficult to start again after an injury. I was out for two months or more, and it's difficult to start again because everybody is playing well, everybody is full of confidence and I just start this year, two weeks before, but I think it's already too late to play tennis, but I'm trying.

Q: I'm just wondering, what is the thing that stands out in your mind, in your memory, when you think back to winning the U.S. Open last year? What's the one thing that comes to mind?

SAFIN: I think that moment is the best moment of my life. I'm thinking right after the match point, I think it's the best memory that I have. It's like you feel so good and so -- the moment is just perfect. You've just won one of the biggest tournaments in the world. It's a Grand Slam tournament and you just won it two seconds ago and you feel so good. It's a good feeling.

Q: I was just wondering, given the success you had last year and everything and then struggling as you had this year because of problems that you couldn't control, how have you dealt with all of those frustrations?

SAFIN: It's tough to live with it. Everybody is playing great and you aren't playing well. People don't respect you anymore like a player. It's a difficult time, but you have to survive somehow and you have to keep working, keep trying, keep playing tournaments and one day it will come. And I'm just hoping the next year I will be 100 percent. The first of January I will be perfect. I'll be healthy and I can start the year like a normal person, like a normal player, not in the middle of the year or the season.
