06.21.2002 Roehampton, UK
Two hours after missing out on a place at Wimbledon, Dinara Safina could not stop crying. The 16-year-old sister of Marat Safin, the No 2 seed in the men’s championship, idly dipped a chip in a pool of tomato ketchup and smeared it around her plate. She barely had the stomach to eat it.
Safina had lost 4-6, 6-2, 8-6 in heart-wrenching circumstances against Zuzani Ondraskova in the final round of qualifying at Roehampton. The opportunity of appearing in her first grand-slam tournament had slipped away. It was particularly galling because she had served for the match at 5-3 and then squandered a match point at 6-5. Failing so narrowly is hard to bear, whatever the stage of your career.
“It’s so tough,” Safina, who was runner-up at Wimbledon in the junior event last year, said. “It was the same last week in Tashkent. I lost again having had match point. I really wanted to play at Wimbledon. I would not have minded if I had lost two and two. But when I was serving for the match I started to make unnecessary mistakes. I had everything in my favour but, to be fair, she (Ondraskova) played a great match, but I should have won.”
It was of little consolation that Rausa, her mother, who was Safin’s coach as a boy, told her that she would learn from the experience. Safina was 16 only in April but, like so many girls from Russia and the former Eastern bloc on the circuit, she is driven by the desire to succeed. Winning equals status, equals prosperity. The equation is simple.
Safin, the US Open champion in 2000 and a Wimbledon quarter-finalist last year, has been an inspiration and also her patron. His financial support pays for his sister and her coach to travel the world from their base in Valencia. Being on the road is expensive, but Safin’s generosity ensures that she can stay in decent hotels, enabling her to concentrate on tennis. “I am lucky to have Marat. But I don’t want him to sponsor me for ever,” she said.
“I want to succeed on my own and earn my own money, maybe even pay him back. I also want to play mixed doubles with him at Wimbledon.”
She took up tennis at the age of seven, Misha, her father, being the director of a small tennis club in Moscow. In theory, she is still supposed to be studying at school, but education has taken a back seat. She returns home today to prepare for her next tournament in Casablanca in two weeks’ time. “My ambition is to win my next match. To succeed you have to fight for every chance.” Will she be back? “Of course,” she said, at last allowing herself a flicker of a smile.

04.08.2002 Estoril, Portugal
Russian teen Dinara Safina makes her WTA Tour main draw debut Monday against third seed Martina Sucha of Slovakia in the first round of the $665,000 Estoril Open.
Safina is the younger sister of ATP Tour star Marat Safin and is coached by their mother, Rausa Islanova. Like her brother, Dinara possesses a big serve, heavy groundstrokes and a volatile temperament.
The 15-year-old from Moscow had a strong junior career, highlighted by a runner-up showing in the girls' singles at Wimbledon in 2001.
Safina finished in the top 20 in the world junior rankings last year and was the runner-up in the girls' doubles at the 2001 French Open.
Earlier this year, Safina won two singles titles and a doubles crown in a pair of International Tennis Federation events.

July 8, 2001 Wimbledon, UK
Dinara Safina was forced to dig deep during the Girls' Singles Final and, in so doing, showed that she could adopt a more subtle approach than simply power although that remains her game's trademark.
Unfortunately for the 15-year-old Russian, she was unable to contain the more consistent and varied play of her opponent, Angelique Widjaja of Indonesia, 16, who collected her first Junior Grand Slam title with a 6-4 0-6 7-5 victory.
Trailing 3-2 when rain suspended play on Saturday, Widjaja, the No. 8 seed, returned to the fray with a better game plan. With some clever play, no pace and sheer consistency, she gained the advantage in the ninth game when a flustered Safina delivered two double faults to concede her serve.
It was then a simple matter of serving out, a feat that Widjaja achieved comfortably to love to take a set lead.
Dropping that set, only her second of the week, spurred Safina, the No. 15 seed, to new heights of purpose. Adopting much the same tactics as her opponent, she quickly turned the tables to level at one set all 18 minutes later.
The contest settled somewhat during the decider as Safina reverted to type, blasting her ground strokes. It was not surprising, therefore, to find Widjaja gaining the ascendancy as she followed the same tactics as the first set.
The result was a break in the seventh game, leaving Safina with plenty of hard work to do to save the match. She recovered with some sensible play to break back when Widjaja first served for the title. But then she failed to hold her own serve and hung her head when the Indonesian achieved her winning objective second time around with a low cross court volley.

July 6, 2001 Wimbledon, UK
Anne Keothavong, the ninth seed from Middlesex, felt the full weight of the Safina power as she was overwhelmed by Marat Safin's 15-year-old sister, Dinara, in straights sets in the Girls' Singles Semi-Finals.
It was a hard-hitting exchange between the two youngsters who prefer to play the game from behind the baseline but Keothavong, with her heavily strapped right thigh, seemed able to match her six-foot Russian opponent.
They exchanged breaks in the early part of the match but then the Safina power took over as she blasted her big shots to good effect to take the next seven games to win the first set 6-2 and take a 4-0 lead in the second.
Keothavong prevented a second set whitewash when she hit back to break 15th seed Safina in the fifth game but it was all too late as she slumped to a 6-2 6-1 defeat.

July 5, 2001 Wimbledon, UK
Men's Quarter-Finalist Marat Safin's baby sister, Dinara, required 61 minutes to reach the Semi-Finals of the Girls' Singles but her passage wasn't as smooth as expected. Her opponent, Daniela Casanova, suddenly found her range to hold her up during the latter part of the second set.
From the start it was Safina who dictated, breaking her Swiss opponent in the second game on her third break point. It was disastrous five-deuce game for Casanova who failed to hang on to her serve on the three game points she had.
She did level three games later only to falter again under Russian pressure to go 5-4 down and, despite her frantic efforts in the next game, was forced to concede the set.
The second set was evenly balanced as Casanova found her accuracy and length and gained in confidence to such an extent that she made the initial break to love for a 5-3 lead but then failed to capitalise on it as the long-legged Safina came storming back to earn a Semi-Final place against British girl Anne Keothavong.
During the match the 15-year-old Safina showed a tendency for racket-throwing, much in the manner of her brother. "I never break them," she said, "but I do throw them when I am angry. It's good to be like my brother, he has won the US Open and been world No. 1."
Safina, the No. 15 seed, is one of three Russians to have made the last eight of the competition. "We have lots of good girls because we have good coaches," she said, giving reasons for her country's rising success. "We play a lot of team events and always meet up a week before to be coached before the competition."

July 4, 2001 Wimbledon, UK
Dinara Safina of Russia has defeated third seed Claudine Schaul of Luxembourg in straight sets in their 3rd Round Girls' Singles match.
While brother Marat faced Goran Ivanisevic on Centre Court, 15th seed Safina made the most of Court 15, comfortably defeating Schaul 6-3 6-2.
Fifteen-year-old Safina took the initial break to lead 4-3 in the first set. She followed this by holding serve and winning another break to claim the set 6-3.
Both players started off carefully in the second set, until Safina once again made the break to lead 3-1. Schaul appeared to be struggling as Safina increased her lead to 4-1. The Russian quickly finished it off by taking the second set and the match 6-3 6-2.

July 3, 2001 Wimbledon, UK
Russian Dinara Safina, the 15th seed, beat Nicole Pitts of the United States in straight sets 6-2 6-0 in the Girls' Singles Championships.
Fifteen-year-old Dinara Safina, who is the sister of Marat Safin, the Men's No.4 seed, had no trouble in beating the young American who produced some wild hitting at times. Pitts lost her serve at 2-1 to give Safina the advantage and ease 4-1 ahead. The American then pulled back a game but it was not enough and the Russian took the first set 6-2.
The second set saw the Russian cruise through without losing a game to advance into the 3rd Round.
Safina reached the 4th Round of the French Junior Open last month.

June 6, 2000 Paris, France
The dream of a double success for the Safin family at the French Open today ended when Marat Safin’s talented sister Dinara Safina was knocked out in the second round of the junior competition, 6-4, 6-3 by Marie-Eve Pelletier of Canada.
Safina, who turned 14 on April 27, lost in the worst possible way, ending the match with two double faults.
Safina shared the fate of Josely Hewitt, younger sister of Lleyton, who was playing a fourth round game in the men’s field, and Giovanni Lapentti, younger brother of the 1999 Australian Open semifinalist, Nicolas, who were also early casualties in Paris.

Feb. 2000
The most impressive individual on display was the girls' top seed,Dinara Safina, sister of Russian Davis Cup player, Marat Safin.
A powerful long limbed 5'11' blonde, she quickly emerged as the player to beat but few could keep pace with her powerful serving and groundstrokes. And she wasn't averse to coming in and dispatching volleys. She dropped just seven games in three matches as she eased her way into the semi-finals where her compatriot, Marina Viliamovskaya, provided the Moscovite with her only serious challenge. She came through 7-5, 6-4 and the next day clinched the title with the comprehensive defeat of America's Whitney Deason, dropping just two games in the 45 minute match.
