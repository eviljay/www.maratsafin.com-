Russia look for new Kournikova in 13-year-old Safina

MOSCOW, Feb 28 2000

She is just 13 years old but stands 178 cm (5ft 10 ins) and hits the ball at well over 120 kph (75 mph). She is Dinara Safina, the younger sister of Russian Davis Cup player Marat Safin.

After recently winning two prestigious tournaments for up to 14-year-olds back-to-back in Telford, England and Tarbes, France Safina became Europe's top ranked player in her age group.

She is coached by her mother, Rauza Islanova, who was a top player in the former Soviet Union in the 1970s.

Already many Russians are talking of the youngster as the next Anna Kournikova. Agents and managers are also lurking in the background, waiting to sign the promising youngster.

"Dinara is a very talented young player, probably the most talented to come out of Russia in a very long time," said Russia Davis Cup captain Shamil Tarpishchev, who used to play mixed doubles with Islanova in their glory days.

"One thing is for sure, she has already achieved more than Marat at the same age." People who know her well also point to Safina's attitude. When asked a couple of years ago if she wanted to emulate Kournikova, she told stunned reporters: "No, because she hasn't won anything yet."

NO REAL HEROES

Safina, who turns 14 in April, said that while she admires Wimbledon and Australian Open champion Lindsay Davenport, she still does not have any real tennis heroes. "I just want to be myself," she told Reuters.

"Obviously, I want to win all the tournaments where I play, but whatever the outcome, I enjoy playing tennis." And does she ever ask her big brother for advice?

"No, my mother's advice is good enough for me right now," she said.

Safina and her mother have had a busy schedule for the last couple of years. When not playing in a tournament, they commute between Moscow and Valencia, where the family also have a house.

In Moscow, she works twice a day under the watchful eye of her mother in a youth tennis academy.

Tennis, however, has seen its share of promising young players such as Jennifer Capriati who had too much too soon and were burned out at an early age.

"Actually, I've been doing just the opposite, I'm holding her back a little bit," said Islanova.
"We don't want to pit her against 18-year-olds all of a sudden, where she would start losing her matches all the time.

It's a gradual process of working your way up."

However, Safina will no longer be playing against her own age group.

"After she won both the singles and doubles in Telford as well as in Tarbes, I don't think there is any point in doing it any more," her mother said.

"Now, we'll be looking into some good under-16 tournaments where she can again prove herself."

NOT ONLY BETTER BUT BIGGER

"The problem is that she is not only better but she is bigger than most of the girls in her age group and she is still growing pretty fast," Islanova added.

"That's why we have to be careful of injuries, particularly her knees which are not used to wear and tear at this age."

Another problem for Safina is trying to juggle her tennis and school-work. "Well, now you can take correspondence courses and just go to school to take exams," Islanova said.

"The good thing is that most teachers are understanding. They are a bit easier on her than on most other kids," she added.

"And that's the way it should be done, because most likely, Dinara will never need a huge amount of chemistry, physics, and mathematics and make a career out of it.

"I just want her to be a well educated and cultivated person and to enjoy her life."
