Safin's Sister Carrying On Family Tradition

Tuesday, September 4, 2001
by Erik Boland

Dinara Safina answers the questions amicably enough, but it's obvious she's answered them before. She would be happy if she didn't have to answer them again, but that seems unlikely, at least in the near future.

Safina, the 15-year-old sister of 2000 US Open champion Marat Safin, is trying to establish her own tennis identity. However, having a superstar for an older brother, one who is recognized worldwide and is a headliner at whatever tournaments he plays, makes her task difficult. Safina won her first round ITF juniors match Tuesday over Ana Vrijic but her post-match press conference followed an all-too-familiar theme. Namely, what is it like having an international tennis star for a brother?

"It is difficult," said Safina, a native Russian but current resident of Spain who speaks English, Spanish and Russian. "Yes, I would like to be known for myself. Everyone always starts by saying, 'You're his sister, you're his sister.' But, I'm just his sister."

Safina is the No. 15 seed in the US Open juniors tournament but in the past she has had to rely on wild card invitations for some tournaments. In the eyes of some tennis cynics, that created some new questions, but still ones with sibling overtones.

"Everyone would be like, 'You're his [Marat's] sister. You got the wild card because you're his sister.' I don't like that."

This year Dinara's game has helped her end those types of questions. Safina finished as the runner-up in this year's Wimbledon junior championship and, with Tuesday's victory, moved to 29-3 in singles in 2001. Currently, Safina is the 11th-ranked junior singles player in the world.

In Tuesday's match, Safina, after winning the first set 6-2, found herself trailing 5-1 in the second. Rediscovering her powerful serve and striking several key winners for break points, Safina rallied to send the set into a tie-breaker. She controlled the tie-breaker 7-2.

"I was down 5-1 and I was like to myself, 'What are you doing?'" Safina said. "I was a little lucky."

Dinara said she is close to her brother but he rarely comes to see her play. Marat knows that by coming to one of her matches, the few people who are there will spend more time watching him than his sister.

"He doesn't like to watch because it might hurt my concentration," she said.

Dinara is paying close attention to her brother as he tries to defend his Open title. She watched Marat's four-set, fourth-round victory over Thomas Johansson from her hotel room Monday evening, even though it ran late and she had to play early Tuesday.

"I was watching it as it got to the fourth set," she said. "I had to get to sleep and it was like, 'Oh, one more game, one more game.'"

Safina said her game is a work in progress and there are not too many parallels between her style of play and her brother's. She did admit to sharing some of Marat's famous temper, but proudly pointed out, "I've only broken one racket all my life." This is one of what Dinara hopes will be many distinctions between her and Marat in the coming years.
